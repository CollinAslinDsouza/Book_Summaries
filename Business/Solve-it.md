# Solve It: The mindset and tools of smart problem solvers
**by Dietmar Sternad**
---

> ### 💡 The Core Thesis
> Complex problem solving is not a rare genetic talent; it is an engineered discipline combining a proactive psychological mindset with a rigorous, five-step execution process. By actively identifying personal zones of influence, moving past cognitive biases, and structuring solutions from systematic root-cause diagnoses, anyone can transform frustrating setbacks into clear, execution-ready roadmaps.

## 📂 <mark>Part 1: The Problem-Solving Mindset</mark>

<details>
<summary><b>Overview — Achieving Optimal Solutions (Click to expand)</b>
<br>

> Achieving optimal solutions requires a dual framework of smart thinking and smart acting. Before deploying analytical tools, a problem solver must actively cultivate psychological resilience, audit their spheres of control, protect against cognitive biases, and leverage external networks alongside unconscious processing to lay a strong foundation for clear thinking.
</summary>

* **1. Architectural Foundations of Mindset:**
    * **Optimistic Ownership:** Smart problem solvers reject passive, defeatist statements like "There is nothing I can do about it". Instead, they approach friction with structural optimism, operating under the core belief that "there must be a better way to move forward".
    * **The Growth Mindset Protocol:** Abilities, analytical skills, and technical acumen are not fixed traits; they are developed through continuous commitment, targeted learning, and deliberate hard work.
    * **The Four-Step Cognitive Shift:** Transitioning from a rigid, fixed mindset to an active growth orientation requires a structured internal checklist:
        1. *Listen to your inner voice:* Explicitly monitor internal dialogues to identify the exact moments you say you "cannot" do something, locating your personal fixed-mindset friction points.
        2. *Deploy the power of "Not Yet":* Intentionally append the word "yet" to any self-limiting belief (e.g., "I am not good at this... yet"), shifting your perspective from permanent failure to future growth.
        3. *Establish learning goals:* Replace superficial performance targets with concrete learning milestones.
        4. *Acknowledge progressive tracking:* Systematically recognize and record incremental progress over time.
* **2. The Spheres of Influence Audit:** A problem should only be accepted as your personal responsibility if it passes a rigorous three-pronged qualification test:
    * **The Value Test:** Is this problem truly important to your core objectives, and does it directly hinder you from achieving goals you care about deeply? Is it worth spending your finite time and energy on?
    * **The Contribution Test:** Can you take concrete actions that at least contribute to a solution, or can you strategically influence others to take meaningful action?
    * **The Control Test:** Are you focusing your energy on the specific sub-components of the problem that you can directly control with your own attitude and actions?

> ### ⚠️ THE SPHERES OF INFLUENCE AUDIT
> 1. Value Test: Does it block core goals? (Yes)
> 2. Contribution Test: Can you affect the outcome? (Yes)
> 3. Control Test: Can your actions make a change? (Yes)
> **[OWN THE PROBLEM]**

* **3. Subconscious and Collaborative Amplification:**
    * **Unconscious Incubation:** Forcing a solution often yields sub-optimal, linear ideas. Smart problem solvers phrase high-quality questions, step away from active analysis, and intentionally allow the unconscious mind to process the issue during a rest period.
    * **Support Network Mobilization:** Complex problem solving should never be done in isolation. Success depends on intentionally curating and rallying a support team with complementary skills.
* **4. The Critical Error Guardrail:** Problem solvers must actively shield themselves from three common analytical traps:
    * **Misidentifying the Core Issue:** Wasting time, resources, and capital solving the wrong problem.
    * **Idea Favoritism:** Developing an emotional attachment to personal "pet ideas," creating a biased evaluation process.
    * **Misjudging Human Dynamics:** Miscalculating the capabilities, motivations, or intentions of the people involved.
</details>

## 📂 Part 2: The Five-Step Problem-Solving Process

<details>
<summary><b>Overview — Stage Progression (Click to expand)</b>
<br>

> Moving from a problem to an optimized solution is a linear, five-stage journey: Clarify, Causes, Create, Choose, and Commit. This structured progression strips away guesswork, ensures that teams isolate real root causes before ideating, balances logic with intuition during choice selection, and builds an iterative loop for persistent execution.
</summary>

* **Step 1: Clarify — Solve the Right Problem**
    * Before spending time on analysis, you must clearly define the issue at hand. True clarity requires translating messy symptoms into a concise written statement, aligning stakeholder goals, and deliberately reframing the problem from multiple perspectives.
    * **The Problem Statement:** Draft a concise, unambiguous written definition of the exact issue to establish a clear baseline for your team.
    * **Goal Alignment Matrix:**
        * *Internal Alignment:* Explicitly define your personal goals, constraints, and target outcomes.
        * *External Alignment:* Map out and understand the distinct goals, expectations, and motivations of all external stakeholders.
    * **Problem Reframing:** Intentionally rewrite and reframe the initial problem statement from different angles to uncover hidden opportunities and alternative paths.
* **Step 2: Causes — Make the Right Diagnosis**
    * Great problem solvers never patch over surface symptoms; they dig down to locate the root cause. This stage requires gathering objective data, establishing structured hypotheses, and testing assumptions before committing capital to a solution.
    * **Data Aggregation:** Systematically collect objective qualitative and quantitative data regarding the problem's scope, frequency, and impact.
    * **Root-Cause Isolation:** Drill past surface symptoms to identify the true underlying drivers of the issue.
    * **Hypothesis Frameworks:** Construct logical, structured hypotheses and test them against your data to confirm your diagnosis before moving to the design phase.
* **Step 3: Create — Find Promising Solutions**
    * Generating innovative options requires a divergent approach. By temporarily suspending judgment, looking outside your industry for analogous solutions, and deploying reverse brainstorming, you can bypass mental blocks and uncover creative breakthroughs.
    * **Divergent Freedom:** Suspend all immediate judgment, remove artificial constraints, and give your mind the freedom to explore unconventional ideas.
    * **Cross-Domain Cross-Pollination:** Look for proven solutions outside your own industry or specialty. This cross-pollination is achieved through a simple two-step abstraction protocol:
        1. *Abstract the Problem:* Describe your issue using general terms (e.g., reframing a hospital patient transfer as a "fast switchover" process).
        2. *Identify External Masters:* Ask what other industries or fields have already mastered that abstract process (e.g., looking at Formula 1 pit stop crews to optimize hospital room turnovers).
    * **Creative Brainstorming Techniques:**
        * *Standard Collaborative Brainstorming:* Gather a diverse team, encourage them to generate a high volume of ideas, and deliberately build upon each other's concepts.
        * *Reverse Brainstorming:* Flip the core question on its head by asking, "How can we deliberately cause this problem?" Identifying ways to create the failure highlights hidden vulnerabilities and reveals fresh solutions.
    * **Structuring Ideas:** Use visual tools like logic trees and mind maps to group, organize, and structure your ideas into clear paths.
* **Step 4: Choose — Decide on the Optimal Solution**
    * Selecting the best solution requires a balance of logical analysis and intuitive self-reflection. Teams must narrow down their options using clear risk matrices, run critical sanity checks to avoid the sunk cost fallacy, and pass a comprehensive personal "heart check".
    * **Analytical Evaluation:** Filter your raw ideas down to a targeted short list. Weigh the pros and cons of each choice, evaluate their required implementation scale, and chart out explicit best-case and worst-case scenarios.
    * **The Second-Look Guardrails:** Run your leading option through four objective sanity checks before final approval:
        1. *Information Check:* Do we have sufficient, high-quality data supporting this choice, or are we relying on unverified assumptions?
        2. *Sunk Cost Check:* Are we choosing this path simply to justify time, money, or resources we have already spent and cannot recover?
        3. *Sensitivity and "What-If" Check:* How well does this solution hold up if our core assumptions change or conditions worsen?
        4. *No-Regrets Check:* Would we still stand by this decision even if the final outcome fails to achieve perfection?
    * **The Holistic "Heart Check":** Pass your decision through a vital lens of personal values and intuition by asking five core questions:
        1. What am I deeply afraid of in this scenario?
        2. What is the real, ultimate purpose behind taking this path?
        3. Who am I truly doing this for?
        4. How will it feel when I look at myself in the mirror after executing this?
        5. What does my gut or heart say about this direction?
* **Step 5: Commit — Make It Happen**
    * An unexecuted solution is completely worthless. The final stage requires securing explicit alignment from your team, building an iterative "Plan-Test-Review-Learn" cycle, and maintaining the persistent execution needed to adapt on the fly.
    * **The Feedback Loop:** Build a nimble execution framework based on a continuous loop of testing, reviewing, and learning.
    * **Team Alignment:** Secure explicit, actionable commitments from every team member regarding their specific responsibilities and deliverables.
    * **Persistent Execution:** Take immediate action, gather real-world data, learn from early friction points, and maintain the persistence required to drive the solution through to completion.
</details>

## 📂 Part 3: Operational Checklists & Metrics

> [!NOTE]
> Use this framework of core tactical actions to seamlessly transition your strategic conclusions into measurable, low-risk execution items.

| Phase | Core Tactical Action | Operational Checklist & Item Metrics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mindset** | The Growth Dialogue Check | Audit your internal thoughts. Identify a self-limiting belief, append the word "yet", and write down two learning milestones to tackle that specific gap. |
| **Clarify** | The Dual-Goal Alignment Map | Write down a one-sentence problem statement. List your personal constraints side-by-side with the distinct goals of your external stakeholders to check for gaps. |
| **Create** | The Cross-Industry Abstraction | Rewrite your problem statement using abstract language (e.g., use "volume allocation" instead of "warehouse spacing"). Identify two unrelated industries that have mastered that abstract concept and note their best practices. |
| **Choose** | The Sunk Cost / Heart Audit | Run your top solution through a strict choice review. Confirm that you are not chasing a bad path simply to justify past expenses, and verify that the choice directly aligns with your core purpose. |
| **Commit** | The Test-and-Learn Sprint | Break your solution down into a small, low-risk test. Define an immediate action item, establish a clear review date, and assign ownership to adapt the plan based on real-world results. |
